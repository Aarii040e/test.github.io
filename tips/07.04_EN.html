The anatomy of a data hijacking attack

Why one infected device can affect everyone

Hello everyone,

Ransomware, or data hijacking, is currently one of the most critical threats to organisations. Its impact is not limited to a single device: it can compromise servers, applications, databases, internal systems and backups.

The attack often begins with a single entry point, such as a malicious email, an attachment, a fraudulent link or stolen credentials. Once inside, the attacker may move silently through the network before executing the final encryption.

Ransomware does not always act immediately.

In many cases, the attacker first observes, locates critical information and searches for backups. The aim is to increase the impact of the attack and make recovery more difficult.
 

Entry and silent movement

The infection may start on a single employee’s device. From there, the attacker attempts to move through the network, identify relevant systems and gather information before becoming visible.
 

Search for critical information

Attackers often locate databases, corporate applications, ERP systems, shared servers and other key assets. They also pay special attention to backups.
 

The risk to backups

A common tactic is to try to delete or disable backups before encrypting the rest of the information. Without reliable backups, recovery becomes much more difficult.
 

Blocking and extortion

The final attack usually consists of encrypting information and rendering systems unusable, demanding a payment in exchange for a supposed recovery key.

Recommendation

Be cautious with unexpected links, attachments and requests. Protect your credentials, do not reuse passwords and report any unusual behaviour on your device or in your email immediately. In a connected network, protecting one workstation helps protect the activity of the entire organisation.
