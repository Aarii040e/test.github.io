Vulnerability in the age of connected devices

Not only computers can become an entry point

Hello everyone,

Cybersecurity no longer applies only to computers, servers or mobile phones. Today, we live with many network-connected devices, such as cameras, smart TVs, sensors, control systems, vehicles and other equipment that can also become part of the attack surface.

This scenario, commonly known as the age of connected devices or IoT, increases risk because many of these devices do not have the same level of protection, maintenance or monitoring as traditional corporate equipment.

Any connected device can become an entry point.

A seemingly secondary device may be used to access information, listen to conversations, observe spaces or serve as a foothold for other attacks within a network.

 

Beyond the computer

Risks are no longer limited to the PC. Cameras, smart TVs, sensors, appliances, industrial systems and modern vehicles include software, connectivity and services that may contain vulnerabilities.
 

Lack of specific protection

While corporate devices usually have antivirus, updates and centralised controls, many connected devices do not receive the same level of attention. This can make them easier targets for an attacker.
 

Physical and privacy risks

Some connected devices include cameras, microphones, sensors or the ability to act on the physical environment. If compromised, they may enable spying, information collection or interference with device functions.
 

Physical protection also matters

Software can fail or be manipulated. Therefore, simple measures such as covering a camera when it is not in use or avoiding sensitive conversations near connected devices can add an extra layer of protection.

Recommendation

Treat any connected device as a potential risk point. Avoid connecting unauthorised equipment to corporate networks, keep approved devices updated and physically cover cameras when they are not in use. Security does not end with the computer.
