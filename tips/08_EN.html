Extortion fraud and family impersonation

Not everything they claim to know is true

Hello everyone,

Some current frauds do not aim to technically compromise a system, but to trigger a quick emotional reaction. Lies, fear, shame or urgency are used so that the victim does not stop to think or verify.

Two especially common types are fake sextortion and family impersonation through SMS or WhatsApp. In both cases, the attacker tries to create alarm in order to obtain a payment, a transfer or an immediate response.

Fear and urgency are part of the deception.

If a message demands payment within a short time, secrecy or action without verification, it is advisable to stop, avoid responding and verify through another channel.
 

Fake sextortion

The attacker sends mass emails claiming to have accessed the account, to know a password or to have recorded the person through the webcam. In most cases, the message is completely false and aims to generate fear in order to obtain payment.
 

Threat of disclosure

These messages often demand urgent payment, frequently in cryptocurrency, under the threat of sending a supposed video or compromising information to the victim’s contacts.
 

Family impersonation

In the “child in trouble” scam, the attacker writes from an unknown number claiming to have changed phone and urgently needing money. The goal is for the victim to act before checking whether they are really speaking to their relative.
 

Automation and artificial intelligence

These scams may rely on automated messages, public information or artificial intelligence techniques to make the conversation appear more real, close and personalised.

Recommendation

If you receive an extortion message, do not pay or respond. Stay calm, block the sender if appropriate and report it through the established channels. If you receive an urgent money request from a relative using a new number, always call the usual number you already had saved before making any transfer. Calm verification breaks the deception.
