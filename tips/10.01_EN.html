Password security and access control

How to protect our digital identity

Hello everyone,

Passwords remain one of the main barriers protecting our digital identity. However, on their own they are no longer enough: they can be guessed, stolen, leaked or reused across other services, opening the door to unauthorised access.

Attackers use automated techniques, dictionaries of common words, contextual user data and large computing capacity to test combinations. For this reason, a predictable or reused password can quickly become a risk for the entire organisation.

A weak or reused password can compromise much more than one account.

Protecting access is not only about choosing a difficult password, but also about not sharing it, not storing it insecurely and reinforcing it with multi-factor authentication.
 

Predictable passwords

Avoid easy-to-guess data such as birth dates, licence plates, company names, cities or the current year. Dictionary attacks specifically test common combinations and contextual information.
 

Do not share credentials

Passwords are personal and non-transferable. They should not be shared with colleagues, suppliers or IT support. No one should need to ask for a password to carry out a legitimate task.
 

Do not reuse passwords

Using the same password at work and in personal services, such as social networks or external platforms, increases the risk. If one of those platforms suffers a data breach, the attacker may try to access corporate systems as well.
 

Secure storage

Passwords should not be stored on post-it notes, visible notebooks, Word documents, Excel files or unprotected files. These locations are easy to find if a device becomes compromised.

Recommendation

Use long passwords, preferably passphrases that are easy to remember and difficult to guess. Always enable and respect multi-factor authentication when available: even if a password is stolen, MFA adds an additional barrier. To manage many passwords, use approved password management tools and avoid storing them in documents or browsers without corporate control.
