The evolution of cyber threats

From anecdotal impact to economic and operational risk

Hello everyone,

Cyber threats have evolved significantly over the last few decades. Understanding this change is important in order to properly assess current risks and strengthen preventive behaviour in the daily use of information systems.

In their early stages, some computer viruses from the 1980s, such as the well-known “Ping Pong virus” in MS-DOS environments, were mainly disruptive or inconvenient. Although they could affect the operation of computers, in many cases they did not pursue direct financial gain.

During the 1990s and early 2000s, threats such as Michelangelo or I Love You gained significant public attention. In many cases, their impact was linked to notoriety, mass distribution and media visibility.

The current context is different.

Today’s threats are often driven by financial interests, executed through more advanced techniques and aimed at compromising information, operations or organisational resources.

 

From experimental use to organised cybercrime

Today, many threats do not come from isolated actions, but from organised groups using structured methods and defined objectives.

 

Financial motivation

A significant part of today’s attacks seeks financial gain through information theft, extortion, fraud, service disruption or pressure on affected organisations.

 

Impact on organisations

The consequences may affect information confidentiality, operational continuity, corporate reputation and the ability to respond to incidents.

Recommendation

When receiving unexpected emails, links, attachments or requests, it is advisable to act with caution and verify their legitimacy before interacting with them. Individual prevention directly contributes to collective security.
