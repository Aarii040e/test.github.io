CEO fraud and advanced social engineering

When urgency and confidentiality can be a trap

Hello everyone,

CEO fraud is a type of attack in which a criminal impersonates a person with authority within the organisation in order to request urgent payments, transfers or financial actions. Its effectiveness does not depend only on technology, but also on psychological pressure and prior knowledge of the victim.

In many cases, attackers study the context before acting: travel, absences, internal conversations, suppliers, responsibilities and approval processes. The goal is to send the request at the moment when it is most difficult to verify.

Extreme urgency and absolute confidentiality are warning signs.

When a request demands immediate action, asks that it not be discussed with anyone or attempts to bypass a usual procedure, it should be treated as a potential fraud attempt until it is verified through a secure channel.
 

What does it involve?

The attacker impersonates a senior manager or executive to request an urgent transfer from someone with payment authority or access to financial processes.
 

Sophistication and timing

Criminals may use real information, such as travel, meetings or absences, to choose the moment when the impersonated person will be difficult to contact.
 

Language of fraud

These messages often combine urgency, confidentiality and pressure: “do it now”, “this is confidential” or “do not tell anyone”. This combination should always trigger additional verification.
 

New threats with AI

Artificial intelligence tools can make it easier to impersonate voice or image in calls and meetings. Therefore, it is not enough to trust an incoming call or a received link: verification must be initiated through a known and reliable channel.

Recommendation

For any exceptional payment request, bank account change or urgent transfer, always follow a double-verification process. If the order arrives by email, verify it by phone by calling a previously known and trusted number yourself. Do not use the phone number, link or contact included in the suspicious message. Procedures exist to protect the organisation and should not be bypassed due to pressure or urgency.
