The evolution of phishing and the rise of smishing

Why we should not trust received links

Hello everyone,

Identity theft attacks have evolved significantly. In the past, they were mainly associated with banking fraud attempts, but today one of their main objectives is to steal work credentials, such as email accounts, access to internal platforms or corporate systems.

These attacks no longer arrive only by email. As corporate filters improve, attackers increasingly use SMS, a technique known as smishing, taking advantage of the fact that mobile phones often have fewer controls and smaller screens where it is harder to review a web address.

The link is the critical point of the deception.

A fake page may look identical to the real one and use an almost identical domain, changing one letter or using an address that is difficult to detect at first sight. That is why the best defence is not to access services through received links.

 

New target: credentials

The objective is no longer only to obtain money directly. Stealing corporate credentials can provide access to email, internal platforms, ERP systems or sensitive organisational information.

 

Cloned pages

Attackers can copy login pages from well-known services, such as Microsoft platforms or banks. The difference is usually in the domain, with minimal changes that may go unnoticed.

 

Smishing: phishing by SMS

Fake SMS messages are especially dangerous because they can appear in apparently legitimate message threads and because it is harder to properly check the destination address on a mobile screen.

 

The small payment trap

A small payment notice, such as a one-euro fee for a retained package, may simply be a hook to capture full card details or redirect the user to a fraudulent website.

Recommendation

Do not access sensitive services through links received by email, SMS or messaging apps. If you receive a notice from a bank, supplier, delivery company or corporate platform, open the browser and type the official address you already know yourself. If in doubt, wait and ask before entering credentials or bank details.
