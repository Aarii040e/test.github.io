INFORMATION TIP

Email and the macro trap

Do not enable content unless you are completely sure

Hello everyone,

Email remains one of the main entry points for cyberattacks in organisations. Among the most dangerous attachments are some Office documents, such as Word or Excel files, that try to convince the user to enable macros or active content.

Macros are small programs embedded in a file. They can have legitimate uses, but they can also be used by attackers to execute malicious actions on the device. Therefore, when a document asks to enable them without a clear reason, we should be suspicious.

Enabling macros may give direct permission to the attacker.

If a file appears blank, locked or displays instructions to “enable content”, it should not be accepted automatically. That click may start the infection.

 

The attachment threat

Word, Excel or other document files may contain macros capable of executing actions on the device. An apparently normal attachment may hide malicious code.

 

Social engineering

Attackers often display blank, blurred or locked documents to convince the user to click “enable content” or “enable macros”. The goal is for the user to activate the threat themselves.

 

Compromised known contacts

Not all dangerous emails come from strangers. A regular customer, supplier or contact may have been infected and may send malicious files without knowing it.

 

Antivirus is not infallible

Security tools can detect many threats, but not all of them. User caution remains an essential barrier before opening attachments or enabling content.

Recommendation

Do not enable macros or active content in documents received by email unless you are completely sure of their origin and necessity. If a file from a known contact seems unusual, verify it through another channel before opening it or activating features. If in doubt, ask through the established channels before clicking.
