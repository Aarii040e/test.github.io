The ransomware turning point

Why a single error can affect an entire network

Hello everyone,

Ransomware marked a turning point in modern cybersecurity by showing that an incident can move from affecting a single device to compromising entire networks in a very short time. This type of threat can not only encrypt information, but also stop an organisation’s activity.

Cases such as WannaCry in 2017 or the attack suffered by Norsk Hydro in 2019 show how an infection can spread rapidly, force systems to be shut down and generate very significant operational and economic impacts.

A single entry point can affect an entire network.

Modern threats can exploit vulnerabilities, credentials or human errors to spread internally and turn a localised incident into a widespread shutdown.

 

WannaCry as a paradigm shift

WannaCry showed that ransomware could spread automatically between devices on the same network. This forced many organisations to stop systems, isolate computers and apply emergency patches to contain the infection.
 

Operational shutdown

A ransomware attack is not limited to data theft or encryption. It can also prevent the use of applications, servers and devices needed to maintain the organisation’s daily operations.
 

Impact on major corporations

In 2019, Norsk Hydro suffered an attack that affected plants and operations worldwide for several weeks, demonstrating that an infection can have far-reaching industrial and economic consequences.
 

Significant economic costs

Norsk Hydro reported losses of more than 36 million euros. Beyond the direct figure, these incidents often generate additional costs related to recovery, operational interruption, technical support and loss of productivity.

Recommendation

Before opening attachments, accessing links or entering credentials, it is advisable to verify the source and legitimacy of the request. With threats capable of internal propagation, one cautious action can prevent an incident from affecting the rest of the network.
