The real cost of a cybersecurity incident

From technical inconvenience to economic and operational impact

Hello everyone,

For years, many computer viruses were perceived as a nuisance for the user: they slowed down devices, caused errors or required occasional cleanup. However, the current impact of a cybersecurity incident can be much more serious.

Today, an attack can paralyse systems, stop processes, prevent production or interrupt service delivery for days or even weeks. For this reason, cybersecurity should not be understood only as a technical matter, but as a key element for business continuity.

The cost of an incident is not limited to IT damage.

Consequences may include direct financial losses, extortion, operational disruption, system recovery, productivity loss and reputational impact.

 

Evolution of impact

In the past, many incidents mainly affected a specific device or user. Today, an attack can compromise complete systems and directly affect the continuity of the organisation.
 

Direct fraud

Some attacks seek to manipulate people into making payments, changing bank account details or transferring money to fraudulent recipients. An apparently legitimate email can cause a direct financial loss.
 

Ransomware extortion

Ransomware is designed to encrypt information or block systems and demand payment in exchange for restoring access. In some cases, the requested amounts can be very high.
 

Cost of downtime

Even when no direct payment is made, a system shutdown can prevent production, customer service, order management or service delivery. This downtime can generate a very significant economic impact.

Recommendation

Before opening attachments, accessing links, approving payments or entering credentials, it is advisable to review the source, context and legitimacy of the request. A small verification step can prevent a technical incident from becoming an economic and operational crisis.
